# Recovery 2 Character World

**Status:** `in_review`
**Approval state:** not approved
**Scope:** original character cast, teaching roles, world logic, voice, behavior, and lesson choreography for Alonso Academy
**Phase owner:** Recovery 2 - Instructional design, character world, and art direction

This document proposes the character and world direction for Alonso Academy. It is a design contract, not a claim that assets, production screens, audio, or lessons already exist. Nothing here becomes approved curriculum or child-visible content without explicit parent review.

## Experience promise

Alonso enters a warm illustrated place where English has an immediate purpose: greet a friend, find an object, follow an action, notice a sound, or help finish a tiny story. Characters do not stand beside worksheets. They model the language, demonstrate the response, react to the attempt, supply a scaffold, and make the next action understandable without requiring Alonso to read English.

The emotional tone is adventurous, affectionate, and capable. It should feel made for a six-year-old, never babyish. Calm means predictable pacing and low pressure, not visual emptiness.

## The world: Luma Landing

Luma Landing is a tactile illustrated archipelago connected by short stepping-stone routes and a small sail ferry. Each island-place owns a clear learning purpose. Moving through the world helps Alonso know what kind of English action comes next.

| Place | Learning purpose | Character lead | Visual memory cue | Typical child action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Welcome Dock** | Greeting, lesson orientation, and familiar-language re-entry | Miko and Nia | Teal signal flags, a round arrival bell, footprints on timber | Wave, listen, greet, choose today's mission |
| **Action Grove** | Verbs, gesture support, movement, and total physical response | Pippa | Coral ribbons winding through oversized leaves and stepping stones | Stand, point, tap, move, copy an action |
| **Sound Workshop** | Careful listening, sound contrast, mouth cues, and letter-sound noticing | Miko | Teal listening tubes, resonant shells, paper sound-waves | Listen, compare, repeat, match a sound |
| **Story Camp** | Short contextual dialogue, listening stories, sequencing, and retell | Nia | Golden lanterns, cloth tent panels, a low story table | Listen, answer a character, choose what happens, retell |
| **Word Gallery** | Retrieval, maintenance review, and a visible collection of understood words | Moss | Moss-green display niches, object plinths, pinned picture labels | Find, name, sort, revisit, use a known word |

The islands are not a level map or reward economy. They are a stable mental model. A location opens because the parent published an appropriate lesson, not because Alonso earned currency, maintained a streak, or beat a score.

## Shared cast DNA

Miko, Pippa, Moss, and Nia are original **Luma companions** with animal-adjacent fantasy designs. Pippa has deliberately birdlike cues and Moss has deliberately turtlelike cues, but none is a conventional real-world species depiction or a variation on an existing entertainment property. Their specific silhouettes, markings, clothing, props, personalities, and teaching behaviors belong to Luma Landing. They share a handcrafted illustrated construction:

- simple, immediately distinct silhouettes that remain recognizable at 64 pixels;
- visible eyes, eyebrows, and mouths large enough to communicate listening and speaking cues;
- soft asymmetry and small hand-made irregularities rather than perfect geometric symmetry;
- matte color, dry-brush edges, paper/felt texture, and restrained linework;
- hands or gesture surfaces that can clearly point, count, cup an ear, wave, and demonstrate actions;
- feet and body weight that make movement readable without frantic animation;
- one role color per character, always paired with shape, pose, and spoken cues so color is never the only signal.

Their proportions are companion-like rather than toddler-like: heads are expressive but not enormous, bodies can perform meaningful gestures, and faces avoid generic emoji expressions.

## Miko - teal sound-scout guide

### Role

Miko is Alonso's primary guide and the owner of careful listening, sound modeling, lesson orientation, and safe re-entry after interruption. Miko introduces why a phrase matters, demonstrates the first model, and makes silence feel acceptable rather than wrong.

### Original silhouette

Miko is an original teal animal-adjacent sound scout with a compact upright body, two large sail-like ears, a cream muzzle and belly, a navy explorer vest, a coral field pouch, and a forked fin-like tail. The ears make listening readable at small sizes; the forked tail settles when the room should become quiet. The silhouette is round at the center with two unmistakable upward listening shapes.

### Personality and movement

- Curious, observant, patient, and quietly delighted by discovery.
- Leans toward a sound before responding.
- Uses one clear gesture at a time.
- Pauses visibly after speaking so Alonso has processing time.
- Celebrates communication with a warm nod, bright eyes, or a small signal-tail pulse—not confetti or exaggerated cheering.

### Teaching ownership

- Gives the first spoken model in clear American English.
- Uses a cupped-ear pose to mean “listen first.”
- Uses a mouth-focus close-up only when a sound cue is instructionally relevant.
- Repeats at normal pace, then once at a deliberately clear pace when support is requested.
- Returns Alonso to the exact activity after a pause, reload, or provider fallback.

### Voice

Warm, lightly adventurous, and unhurried. Short sentences; concrete verbs; no baby talk. Miko never says “easy,” “you should know this,” or “wrong.”

Example: “Listen first. Hello.” `[pause]` “Now you can wave and say hello.”

### Must not

- narrate every interface change;
- become a decorative logo that watches while text explains the task;
- judge accent or demand accent-perfect imitation;
- speak over Alonso's recording turn;
- use a telescope, explorer hat, or other stock “adventure mascot” shorthand.

## Pippa - coral kinetic action and gesture guide

### Role

Pippa turns language into visible body meaning. She owns action modeling, gesture-supported vocabulary, movement breaks, and energetic recovery when sitting and listening have become too demanding.

### Original silhouette

Pippa is an original coral birdlike action guide with a swept crest, expressive wing-arms, a navy neckerchief and pouch, and a springy body that compresses before movement and opens into a strong readable action line. The wing-arms must retain clear pointing, framing, opening, closing, and whole-body gesture silhouettes rather than behaving as decorative flapping wings.

### Personality and movement

- Bold, playful, and precise rather than chaotic.
- Demonstrates one verb with a beginning, middle, and held final pose.
- Freezes at the key meaning moment so a child can inspect the action.
- Invites imitation with an open palm and sufficient wait time.
- Uses humor through physical timing, never through failure or embarrassment.

### Teaching ownership

- Models actions such as sit, stand, point, wave, open, close, and touch.
- Supplies a gesture before a translation whenever the meaning is imageable.
- Leads short purposeful movement breaks that preserve the current attempt state.
- Reduces a difficult choice to two visibly distinct actions when the lesson's hint ladder permits it.

### Voice

Bright, rhythmic, and concise. Pippa speaks in action-sized chunks and leaves silence for movement.

Example: “Stand up.” `[models and holds]` “Your turn.”

### Must not

- bounce continuously or create visual noise while Alonso is listening;
- equate speed with success;
- turn movement into points, races, streaks, or competition;
- use gestures that are culturally ambiguous when a clearer action is available.

## Moss - moss-green steady meaning and object guide

### Role

Moss makes meaning concrete. Moss owns object vocabulary, picture grounding, category cues, careful choice comparison, and the Word Gallery. When a spoken prompt is unfamiliar, Moss shows what it refers to without replacing the English interaction with dense explanation.

### Original silhouette

Moss is an original moss-green turtlelike meaning guide with a broad low center of gravity, a leaf-pattern shell, and an ochre satchel made to hold picture cards. His hands form clear framing shapes around an object or card. His silhouette is horizontal, grounded, and shell-led, contrasting Miko's sail-like ears and Pippa's diagonal wing-arm movement.

### Personality and movement

- Steady, resourceful, kind, and gently funny.
- Looks at the object before naming it so joint attention is obvious.
- Places, sorts, rotates, or frames one object at a time.
- Responds to uncertainty by simplifying the scene, not by adding more language.

### Teaching ownership

- Establishes picture and object meaning before listening discrimination.
- Presents plausible, visually distinct options with stable semantic IDs.
- Removes irrelevant scene clutter during remediation.
- Revisits familiar objects in the Word Gallery for spaced retrieval.
- Indicates “same,” “different,” “here,” and “not this one” through object placement plus speech.

### Voice

Grounded, friendly, and matter-of-fact. Slightly lower energy than Pippa, with natural conversational phrasing.

Example: “This is the cup.” `[frames the cup]` “Find the cup.”

### Must not

- present a shelf of disconnected flashcards as a finished lesson;
- reveal the correct choice through glow, size, position, or gaze before Alonso answers;
- use text labels as the primary meaning cue;
- collect words as trophies or imply that a smaller collection is failure.

## Nia - golden conversational and story partner

### Role

Nia gives English a social reason. She owns short conversational turns, micro-stories, retell support, contextual choice, and warm lesson closure. Nia needs information from Alonso; she is a partner in communication, not another evaluator.

### Original silhouette

Nia is an original golden cometlike conversation and story partner with a tall luminous body, a navy capelet, a star marking on her cheek, and open crescent hands. Her trailing comet shape gives dialogue and story scenes a gentle directional flow without emitting glossy light effects. The gold is carried through flat layered shapes and paper cutouts.

### Personality and movement

- Warm, imaginative, socially attentive, and comfortable waiting.
- Uses eye line and turn-taking poses to show who is speaking.
- Reacts to meaning rather than accent perfection.
- Makes a story problem small, concrete, and solvable through the approved target language.

### Teaching ownership

- Creates a genuine reason for a greeting, request, answer, or retell.
- Holds a listening pose through Alonso's turn without impatient looping motion.
- Accepts approved response variants and recasts gently when appropriate.
- Uses two- or three-picture story beats for supported retell.
- Ends with a communication-centered reflection: what Alonso understood or helped accomplish.

### Voice

Expressive but natural, with a conversational American English model. Emotion is conveyed through phrasing, not theatrical overacting.

Example: “I found the red bag. What did you find?”

### Must not

- ask open-ended questions beyond the approved vocabulary boundary;
- pretend to understand an unavailable or low-confidence transcript;
- praise pronunciation as “perfect”;
- become a princess, fairy, or magical-girl archetype through costume or behavior.

## Required pose and expression families

Every pose must communicate an instructional state. Decorative idle poses do not count toward the required set.

| Character | Required poses | Required expressions | Instructional use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Miko | arrive/wave, neutral guide, cup-ear listen, model-speak, clear-slow model, mouth cue, wait, retry invitation, resume, quiet celebration | curious, attentive, encouraging, gently puzzled, proud-with-restraint | Orientation, audio model, sound cue, recovery, feedback |
| Pippa | ready stance, point, wave, sit, stand, touch, open, close, copy-me, freeze-frame, movement-break lead | eager, focused, playful, calm-after-motion | Gesture vocabulary, action choice, TPR, break |
| Moss | present object, frame object, compare two, sort, remove distractor, point to scene, gallery place, gallery retrieve | thoughtful, certain, inviting, curious | Meaning establishment, selection, remediation, retrieval |
| Nia | greet, listen to partner, speaking turn, question, story-present, react-to-meaning, sequence first/next/last, retell invitation, closure | welcoming, interested, surprised, concerned, relieved, warmly pleased | Dialogue, story, retell, contextual exit |

### Expression limits

- No red angry face for an incorrect response.
- No tears, shame, alarm, or disappointment as instructional feedback.
- No uncanny mouth morphs. Sound cues use reviewed mouth shapes tied to actual instruction.
- Eyes never track the correct answer before a choice.
- “Try again” uses attentive eyebrows, a neutral-soft mouth, and an open gesture.

## Character-led Phase A lesson choreography

The standard oral-first arc is a handoff among characters, not a sequence of text cards.

| Step | Character behavior | Alonso behavior | Evidence intent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Greeting and context | Miko welcomes Alonso at the relevant Luma Landing location; Nia or Moss establishes a simple need | Watches, waves, or makes one low-risk familiar response | Entry/readiness only; no mastery inference |
| 2. Model | Miko gives one clear spoken model with a visible referent or action | Listens; replay remains available | Listen count and support history |
| 3. Meaning | Moss frames the object/scene or Pippa demonstrates the action | Connects sound with picture or movement | Meaning exposure, not scored success |
| 4. Guided imitation | Miko or Pippa models, waits, and offers a non-punitive retry/fallback | Echoes, gestures, or selects an approved fallback | Supported response, separately labeled |
| 5. Listening discrimination | Moss presents plausible visual options while Miko speaks without looking toward the answer | Chooses the matching picture/action | Server-scored selection and first attempt |
| 6. Contextual spoken turn | Nia needs a greeting, request, answer, or tiny story contribution | Speaks or uses the approved semantic fallback | Provider- or server-derived response evidence |
| 7. Spaced retrieval | The target reappears later in a different place, speaker, or object arrangement | Retrieves after time/context change | Retrieval context and support state |
| 8. Independent exit | A character presents a brief meaningful need without pre-cueing the answer | Responds independently or exposes insufficient evidence | Valid exit evidence; never completion-by-presence |

## Turn-taking rules

1. Only one character gives the instruction for a turn.
2. A second character may create context but becomes still and silent during the prompt.
3. The speaking character visibly finishes, settles into a listening pose, and waits.
4. Audio, pose, scene, and interaction affordance must agree on the same requested action.
5. Feedback reacts to the semantic response; it never invents what Alonso said.
6. When the provider is unavailable, the character acknowledges the technical pause in child-safe language and presents the approved picture/action fallback.
7. A pause or navigation return restores the same character, pose family, scene, options, support level, and turn state.

## Help and remediation behavior

Characters provide the least help necessary and make support visible in the evidence model.

| Support stage | Character action | What changes | What does not change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Independent | Gives the normal prompt, then waits | Nothing | Answer set, target, evidence identity |
| Replay | Miko repeats the approved audio model | Listen history increments | Correctness and first-attempt history |
| Prompted | Character adds one gesture or points to the relevant scene region | One meaning cue | Answer key and target |
| Reduced choices | Moss removes distractors according to the reviewed hint ladder | Visible options reduce | Original attempt record |
| Modeled | Miko/Pippa demonstrates the complete response, then invites participation | Full scaffold | Success remains modeled, never reclassified as independent |

Spanish rescue is brief, optional, and parent-configured. It may clarify the task once (“Escucha y toca la imagen”) but must not translate every target before Alonso has a chance to derive meaning from character, picture, action, and English audio. Rescue use is a support event, not a failure.

## Voice and dialogue standard

- American English model, warm and natural.
- One instruction per turn, usually 3–9 spoken words for Phase A.
- Character name is not repeated in every line.
- Concrete words precede explanations.
- Pauses are authored, not accidental provider latency.
- Captions are optional support for the parent/accessibility layer; Alonso's task cannot depend on reading them.
- No sarcasm, teasing, false urgency, or “Can you say…” when the intended instruction is simply “Say…” or a meaningful conversational prompt.
- No claims about emotion, diagnosis, intelligence, or effort that the system cannot know.

### Feedback language

| State | Approved tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Correct meaning | Specific and brief | “Yes—the cup.” |
| Communication succeeded | Meaning-centered | “I understood you. Hello!” |
| Incorrect selection | Neutral reset | “That is the bag. Listen again: cup.” |
| Silence | Patient and factual | “I did not hear words yet. Take your time.” |
| Provider unavailable | Honest and actionable | “The microphone needs a rest. Show Nia with a picture.” |
| Completion | Reflective, not inflated and response-mode honest | Spoken-use variant: “You used hello with Nia.” Semantic-fallback variant: “Nia understood your greeting.” |

## World continuity without gamification

- The Welcome Dock shows the one parent-published mission, not a carousel of locked content.
- The ferry path indicates lesson sequence, not rank.
- The Word Gallery displays understood and practiced meanings as revisit-able picture moments, not coins or trophies.
- Returning targets appear as familiar objects, characters, and places in new contexts.
- Completion may change a small world detail tied to the lesson story, such as placing Nia's found bag at the dock. It does not trigger loot, random rewards, fireworks, leaderboards, streaks, or loss aversion.
- Character affection is unconditional; it is never withheld for an incorrect response.

## Accessibility and child safety

- Character role, pose, audio cue, and interface affordance redundantly communicate the task.
- Every meaningful pose and scene asset requires concise alt text and a parent-readable description.
- Gesture directions account for left/right ambiguity and avoid requiring fine motor precision.
- Mouth cues are optional support and never the sole instruction.
- Motion can be reduced or replaced by held key poses without losing meaning.
- Character colors meet contrast requirements against their intended scene fields and are never the sole state indicator.
- Faces remain readable for children with varied visual attention; background characters never compete with the active speaker.
- Recording begins only after an explicit child action and a clear Miko/Nia recording state.

## Parent review checklist

Recovery 2 cannot mark this direction approved until the parent explicitly confirms:

- Miko feels original, trustworthy, and appropriate as Alonso's primary guide.
- Pippa, Moss, and Nia have distinct teaching jobs rather than decorative personalities.
- Luma Landing feels exciting and premium without becoming noisy, glossy, or game-like.
- The character-led lesson arc can be understood without reading English.
- Voice language, Spanish rescue, feedback, and celebration rules fit the family.
- The proposed cast and world should proceed to versioned production planning and any parent-requested concept revision.
