# Recovery 2 Instructional Blueprint

## Status and decision boundary

**Status (2026-07-13): proposed for parent review.** This document defines a Recovery 2 instructional and art direction. It is not parent-approved, production-implemented, verified, or pilot-ready. It does not alter curriculum, generate or approve lesson artifacts, publish content, activate a provider, or authorize Recovery 3.

Recovery 2 defines what the learning experience must be before production interfaces and lesson schema v2 are built. The current parent and child interfaces are rejected reference material, not foundations to reskin. No production screen should be rebuilt from this document until the parent explicitly approves the character/world direction and adult-interface direction.

## Product promise

For 12–15 minutes, Alonso enters a coherent illustrated world where a trusted character helps him understand and use a small amount of American English. He hears language before he is asked to respond, sees meaning demonstrated through action and context, practices with decreasing support, retrieves it after a delay, and completes one independent communicative exit.

The experience must feel like a premium children’s application, not a worksheet, technical dashboard, slideshow, or collection of text cards. Calm means predictable and emotionally safe; it does not mean plain, static, colorless, or visually unfinished.

## Source curriculum boundary

This proposal is aligned to the committed A-U1 structural curriculum, **Hello, Listen, and Respond**. It does not assert the hosted unit’s approval state or replace an immutable parent-approved weekly plan.

### A-U1 target inventory used by the concepts

| Target family | Targets | Recovery 2 treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Oral vocabulary | `hello`, `goodbye`, `yes`, `no`, `please`, `thank you`, `listen`, `look`, `point`, `stop` | Taught through character action, social context, object/scene response, and spoken models. Never introduced as written word cards. |
| Oral frames | `Hello!`; `My name is ___.`; `Yes, please.`; `No, thank you.` | Modeled as complete communicative turns. Short approved responses remain acceptable; the character recasts without demanding repetition. |
| Sound anchors | `/m/` with `m`; `/s/` with `s` | Sound recognition only. Mouth/gesture/anchor imagery may be shown; reading is not required. |
| Letter selection | Choose `m` or `s` after hearing its anchor sound | The only planned literacy demand. The child taps one large letter shape after hearing and seeing the sound cue; no typing, spelling, or decoding. |

Structural limits retained by this proposal:

- five lesson days;
- 12–15 minutes per lesson, never over 15;
- no more than three new oral targets in one lesson;
- no reading demand;
- writing limited to letter selection;
- no more than two new sound anchors in the week;
- stories are listening-only;
- translation is support, not the teaching mode;
- completion does not equal mastery;
- independent retrieval must be observed across more than one context before later mastery rules can consider stability.

If an approved curriculum snapshot or weekly plan differs from this proposal, Recovery 3 must fail closed and return the concepts for parent reconciliation. Art direction never overrides curriculum authority.

## Required Phase A learning arc

Every normal lesson follows this order. A shorter 12-minute lesson may compress a beat, but it may not remove or reorder the instructional logic.

| Beat | Typical time | Child experience | Instructional purpose | Evidence authority |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Character greeting and context | 0:00–0:45 | Miko arrives in a location with one clear need or social moment. | Establish safety, attention, and a reason for the language. | Presence only; no scored evidence. |
| 2. Model | 0:45–2:00 | The character says the target naturally, then once in a slightly isolated form. | Provide an accurate American-English model before production. | Required first-listen event; replay remains distinct. |
| 3. Meaning through image/action | 2:00–3:45 | A character gesture, facial reaction, or scene change makes the meaning visible. | Establish comprehension without translation or text. | Optional observation; no correctness inferred from watching. |
| 4. Guided imitation | 3:45–5:15 | Alonso joins a low-pressure echo, gesture, or choice-supported turn. | Build articulatory familiarity without treating echo as independent use. | Modeled/prompted evidence only. |
| 5. Listening discrimination | 5:15–7:15 | He hears one prompt/model and finds the matching action, expression, or scene. | Check receptive meaning and sound discrimination. | First semantic choice is preserved; correctness is server-derived later. |
| 6. Movement reset | 7:15–8:15 | A 30–60 second gesture or whole-body action resets attention. | Regulate pace and rehearse meaning kinesthetically. | Listen/participation state only; never mastery. |
| 7. Contextual spoken turn | 8:15–10:30 | A character speaks directly to Alonso and waits for a meaningful reply. | Move from imitation to communication. | Speech/semantic fallback evidence with exact support level. |
| 8. Spaced retrieval | 10:30–12:15 | After another brief event, the target returns in a changed scene or with a different character. | Test retrieval rather than immediate echo memory. | Separate retrieval context and attempt ordinal. |
| 9. Independent exit | 12:15–14:15 | One new but familiar situation asks for the target without model, highlighted answer, or Spanish cue. | Capture the strongest lesson-level evidence. | Independent first response remains immutable; assisted recovery never overwrites it. |
| 10. Completion and preview | 14:15–15:00 | The world responds to the communication and previews where the path goes next. | End predictably and celebrate effort/meaningful use. | Completion is recorded separately from mastery. |

### Pacing rules

- Spoken directions contain one action and normally no more than eight English words.
- A model is followed by 600–900 ms of silence before the interface expects action.
- A speaking turn allows at least five seconds before any gentle prompt.
- No timed countdown communicates failure. Recording may show a calm preparation count, but the child is never rushed.
- One screen presents one primary action. Repeat and Help remain stable secondary actions in the same positions.
- A choice set begins with two or three semantically distinct images; four is an upper bound.
- The same target is not tested immediately after every model. At least one unrelated beat separates model from independent retrieval.
- New oral targets are distributed across the week; review targets return through changed characters, locations, or communicative purposes.

## Teaching model for a six-year-old beginner

### Meaning before performance

1. Show the situation.
2. Let the character act and speak.
3. Confirm meaning with image, expression, or gesture.
4. Invite Alonso to join.
5. Ask for recognition.
6. Ask for a communicative turn.
7. Return later for retrieval.

The application must never present an unfamiliar English sentence and ask Alonso to infer the task from written text. It must never score pronunciation before meaning and intent are established.

### Target budget

- Default one or two new oral targets in a lesson; maximum three only when the exact parent-approved weekly plan justifies it. A sentence frame counts as a new target when it is explicitly taught, required, scored, or represented as learned. A natural contextual model or recast marked exposure-only does not count as a new target and cannot create target evidence or a learned-target claim.
- One primary communicative target is emphasized at a time.
- Two to four familiar targets may return for review if they do not obscure the primary task.
- A sound anchor occupies no more than 60–75 seconds and does not become a decoding lesson.
- A story may contain connective language for natural narration, but only approved targets can be required, scored, or represented as learned.
- Names of background objects are not spoken merely because they appear in an illustration.

### Gesture and action support

| Function | Stable gesture |
| --- | --- |
| `hello` / `goodbye` | Open hand wave; goodbye adds body turn or departure. |
| `yes` | One clear nod. |
| `no` | Gentle head shake; no scolding expression. |
| `please` | Open hands close to chest, then toward the requested choice. |
| `thank you` | Hand to heart with warm eye contact. |
| `listen` | Character cups one ear and stills its body. |
| `look` | Two fingers move from eyes toward the focus. |
| `point` | Extended wing/paw toward a clearly highlighted object. |
| `stop` | Open palm plus frozen body pose. |
| `/m/` | Closed lips, hand resting lightly on throat/cheek to feel the hum. |
| `/s/` | Teeth close, finger traces a slow air path; no snake stereotype is required. |

Gestures are meaning support, not decorative animation. They must be consistent across lessons and have reduced-motion alternatives using held key poses.

## Support ladder

Support escalates only as needed. Every escalation is recorded separately; a later success does not rewrite the first attempt.

| Level | Name | What changes | Evidence interpretation |
| ---: | --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Independent | New context, no model, no highlight, all valid choices. | Candidate independent retrieval. |
| 1 | Replay | Exact English prompt/model repeats once on child request or after a long pause. | Replay-supported; not independent. |
| 2 | Visual/gesture cue | Character repeats the stable gesture or scene action without naming the answer. | Prompted meaning support. |
| 3 | Modeled cue | Character supplies the target, a first sound, or a sentence starter, then invites a turn. | Modeled/prompted production. |
| 4 | Reduced choice | Distractors reduce to two while the correct meaning remains unhighlighted. | Reduced-choice success. |
| 5 | Spanish rescue | A short Spanish task-orientation cue explains what to do, followed immediately by the exact English task prompt. Supplying the target or answer is a separate modeled-cue escalation, never part of Spanish rescue. | Rescue-supported; never independent evidence. |
| 6 | Safe alternate/stop | The child uses a semantic tap/gesture fallback or exits safely with state saved. | Insufficient evidence for the unavailable mode; no fabricated success. |

### Spanish rescue rules

Spanish rescue exists to restore task understanding, not to translate the lesson or supply an answer.

It may appear when:

- Alonso taps Help;
- there are two unsuccessful meaning attempts;
- there is roughly eight seconds of inactivity after a repeated prompt;
- microphone/provider failure removes the expected response mode;
- a parent-enabled accessibility preference requests it.

It must:

- use one short, warm instruction, normally four to seven Spanish words;
- describe the action, not translate every choice;
- immediately replay the exact English task prompt with the same image/gesture, without adding the target answer; if a full model is later needed, record a separate modeled-cue escalation;
- remain optional and replayable;
- record `spanish_rescue` support without changing first-attempt, correctness, or latency history;
- never appear during the first independent exit prompt unless Alonso requests Help; once requested, the exit is no longer independent.

Approved concept examples:

- “Sigue la demostración.”
- “Elige una imagen.”
- “Responde al personaje.”
- “No te escuché. Intenta otra vez.”
- “Elige una de las dos.”

Spanish copy is provisional until parent review. It must never expose adult settings or technical provider language.

## Remediation and safe recovery

| Situation | Child-facing response | State/evidence rule |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Incorrect first choice | Character reacts neutrally: “Let’s look again.” The scene replays meaning before another choice. | Preserve incorrect first response; increment retry; no automatic advance. |
| Second difficulty | Add stable gesture and reduce to two choices. | Mark prompted/reduced-choice support. |
| Difficulty after scaffold | Character models once, lets Alonso complete the action, and moves forward without a success claim. | Record modeled participation or insufficient evidence; schedule future review later. |
| Silence during speech | “I didn’t hear words yet.” Offer Try again and Show me. | Silence is no response, not incorrect pronunciation. |
| Microphone denied | Character points to a picture/gesture response: “You can show me.” | Preserve lesson state; spoken evidence remains unavailable. |
| Speech provider unavailable | Remove recording as the primary action and expose a semantic choice/gesture fallback immediately. | Provider failure cannot change correctness or complete a scored spoken requirement as speech. |
| Audio missing before lesson | Do not start the learning step. Return to a friendly saved-state screen for a parent to resolve. | No listen event or evidence is created. Production publication must later fail before this state. |
| Image/gesture asset missing | Do not replace it with a word label or broken placeholder. | Block is unavailable; no evidence is collected. Asset validation belongs to Recovery 3. |
| Pause, navigation, or break | Show the current location and guide pose on return; replay the instruction, not the scored response. | Restore exact block, support, retries, first attempt, and view state. |
| Child wants to stop | Save and return to Home without disappointment language. | Attempt remains paused; no completion or mastery event. |

## Evidence semantics

Recovery 2 defines instructional meaning; Recovery 1 remains authoritative for storage and state transitions, and Recovery 3 must encode the approved semantics in schema v2.

### Evidence principles

- **Presentation is not evidence of learning.** Hearing or watching a model proves exposure only.
- **Echo is supported production.** It may show willingness/articulatory participation but cannot be labeled independent use.
- **First attempt is immutable.** Replay, hints, Spanish rescue, reduced choices, and modeled success never overwrite it.
- **Correctness is server-derived.** The browser may submit only a semantic response ID or provider result through an authorized boundary.
- **Support is cumulative.** The strongest support used before the response determines that response’s support level.
- **Context matters.** Character, location, communicative function, and response mode identify whether retrieval transferred.
- **Silence is distinct.** It is neither a wrong pronunciation nor a behavioral failure.
- **Fallback evidence is mode-specific.** A correct picture choice can show comprehension; it cannot prove spoken production.
- **Completion is not mastery.** A child may complete after remediation while the target remains unstable or has insufficient independent evidence.
- **No raw audio is stored.** Only bounded transcript, confidence, timing, provider outcome, and derived evidence may later persist.

### Evidence map by lesson beat

| Beat | Evidence type | Can qualify for lesson completion? | Can later support mastery? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| First model | `modeled_listen` with first-listen/replay distinction | Required exposure where specified, but not sufficient alone | Exposure only |
| Meaning through action | `observed_model` | No | No |
| Guided imitation | `guided_imitation` | Activity participation only | Never independent |
| Listening discrimination | `receptive_choice` tied to semantic option and target | Yes when the approved rubric requires it | Yes, with support/context |
| Contextual spoken turn | `spoken_use` or `semantic_fallback` | According to approved response-mode rubric | Spoken use and fallback remain distinct |
| Spaced retrieval | `retrieval_choice` / `retrieval_spoken_use` | Yes | Stronger when independent and in a changed context |
| Independent exit | `independent_exit` | Yes only when correct evidence is server/provider-derived | Strongest lesson-level evidence; still not mastery alone |
| Story retell | `retell_sequence`, `retell_gesture`, or `retell_spoken_target` | Only for approved story/review lessons | Contextual transfer, not free-form language diagnosis |

### Minimum evidence payload concept

Every later evidence event should trace to:

- child, assignment, immutable lesson artifact, slot/day, attempt and mode;
- block and semantic response/option ID;
- target IDs and evidence rubric version;
- first-attempt ordinal, retry count, support level, and context ID;
- response mode and outcome, including silence/provider-unavailable;
- bounded latency and, for provider speech only, bounded transcript/confidence/model metadata;
- `rawAudioStored: false`.

## Audio direction

This is a voice and sound brief, not provider activation or voice approval.

### Character voice casting

| Character | Voice function | Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Miko | Primary guide, careful-listening host, and American-English model | Warm young-adult voice; clear General American English; curious, never babyish; medium pace around 125–145 words per minute for narration and slower isolated targets. |
| Pippa | Action, gesture, and movement guide | Bright, rhythmic, concise energy; action-sized lines with silence for movement; avoids speed that reduces intelligibility. |
| Moss | Meaning, object, and picture-support guide | Grounded, friendly, and matter-of-fact; slightly lower energy; natural phrasing and generous pauses while framing meaning. |
| Nia | Peer conversation and story partner | Warm, socially attentive, and expressive without theatrical excess; creates a real reason to answer and leads stories at Story Camp. |

The parent must later preview and approve the production voice and retention policy. No voice ID or provider is selected here.

### Audio script rules

- Model natural phrase first, then isolate only the target if needed, then return to natural context.
- Use contractions and rhythm natural to American English; do not distort sounds for scoring.
- Keep action prompts short: “Listen.” “Look at Miko.” “Point to yes.”
- Avoid praise after every tap. Use specific feedback: “You found the wave.”
- Never say “Wrong,” “Bad,” “Easy,” “Perfect accent,” or imply the microphone judges the child.
- Use a soft nonverbal earcon before listening and a different short earcon after a meaningful completed action. Earcons never encode correctness by themselves.
- Captions are optional accessibility support and off by default for Alonso’s Phase A flow. Any caption repeats audio; it never carries unique instructions.
- Every required line needs a reviewed audio asset, text transcript for adults, fallback behavior, locale, voice version, and approval state in the future registry.

## No-reading proof

A concept passes Recovery 2 only if each state meets all of these checks:

1. **Blank-text test:** removing every English word from the child screen does not remove the task’s meaning or primary action.
2. **First-frame test:** before audio, the scene communicates who needs attention and what objects/actions are available; audio and demonstration then explain the exact task.
3. **Demonstration test:** the guide performs one sample with a non-scored item before Alonso is assessed.
4. **Icon stability test:** Repeat, Help, Pause, and recording controls keep consistent icons, location, and sound labels.
5. **No text-choice test:** answer choices use approved scenes, objects, expressions, or gestures—not written English labels.
6. **Audio-loss test:** if essential audio is unavailable, the app pauses safely rather than replacing teaching with text.
7. **Literacy exception test:** only the approved `m`/`s` letter-selection activity displays graphemes as answer content; spoken/visual cues still explain the action.
8. **Parent-preview test:** adult review exposes every spoken line, optional caption, image, action, answer rule, hint, rescue, fallback, and evidence consequence before approval.

## Character and world bible

All names and designs below are original proposals and require parent approval. They must not imitate a branded character’s silhouette, costume, face, signature palette, or world.

### Guide: Miko, the original teal sound-scout creature

- **Role:** trusted primary guide, careful-listening host, American-English model, and safe re-entry companion.
- **Silhouette:** original teal animal-adjacent sound scout with a compact upright body, two large sail-like ears, a cream muzzle and belly, a navy explorer vest, a coral field pouch, and a forked fin-like tail. The ears make listening readable at small sizes; the tail settles when the room should become quiet.
- **Personality:** warm, lightly adventurous, curious, observant, and patient. Miko waits and notices rather than performing constant excitement.
- **Teaching behavior:** faces Alonso for models, turns toward the meaningful object for demonstrations, visibly listens during child turns, and models approved mouth cues for `/m/` and `/s/` without turning them into pronunciation scores.
- **Core poses:** welcome wave, cup-ear listen, model-speak, clear-slow model, mouth cue, waiting/listening, thoughtful hint, quiet celebration, and safe pause.
- **Avoid:** stock explorer-mascot shorthand, jumping, finger wagging, endless bouncing, exaggerated mouth scoring, or decorative placement disconnected from the task.

### Supporting character: Pippa, the coral action guide

- **Role:** movement and gesture guide; hosts action words, total physical response, and attention resets.
- **Silhouette:** original coral birdlike action guide with a swept crest, expressive wing-arms, a navy neckerchief and pouch, and a springy body that compresses before movement and opens into a strong readable action line.
- **Personality:** energetic but not frantic; bright, capable, and focused on clear starts, holds, and stops.
- **Teaching behavior:** demonstrates whole-body meaning, freezes for `stop`, points with a readable action line, and provides movement resets without racing or scoring.

### Supporting character: Moss, the moss-green meaning guide

- **Role:** makes objects, pictures, and story meaning concrete; hosts careful option comparison, remediation, retrieval, and the Word Gallery.
- **Silhouette:** original moss-green turtlelike meaning guide with a broad low center of gravity, a leaf-pattern shell, an ochre picture-card satchel, and hands that form clear framing shapes around an object or picture.
- **Personality:** grounded, friendly, deliberate, and delighted by careful noticing.
- **Teaching behavior:** frames the semantic referent before discrimination, presents stable picture options, removes irrelevant clutter during support, and helps ground Story Camp panels while Nia remains the storyteller.

### Supporting character: Nia, the golden conversation and story partner

- **Role:** peer conversation partner and Story Camp storyteller whose needs create real reasons to greet, answer, choose, introduce oneself, and retell.
- **Silhouette:** original golden cometlike conversation and story partner with a tall luminous body, a navy capelet, a star marking on her cheek, open crescent hands, and a trailing comet shape that guides attention across first/next/last story images.
- **Personality:** welcoming, socially attentive, interested, and warmly expressive without becoming theatrical or evaluative.
- **Teaching behavior:** asks honest choices, waits, accepts both yes and no warmly, responds to Alonso’s intent rather than demanding a perfect form, and leads listening-only micro-stories and retell at Story Camp.

### World: Luma Landing

Luma Landing is a tactile illustrated archipelago connected by short stepping-stone routes and a small sail ferry rather than a menu of disconnected games. Its five stable places each own a learning purpose:

1. **Welcome Dock** — greetings, lesson orientation, familiar-language re-entry, and safe return.
2. **Action Grove** — action words, gesture support, movement, and total physical response.
3. **Sound Workshop** — careful listening, sound contrast, Miko’s mouth cues, and approved letter-sound noticing.
4. **Story Camp** — Nia-led contextual dialogue, listening stories, sequencing, and retell, with Moss grounding objects and panels.
5. **Word Gallery** — Moss-led retrieval, maintenance review, and a visible collection of familiar picture meanings; it never introduces an unpracticed target as a learned collection item.

World progress is represented by characters reaching the next location and collected picture memories—not points, currency, streaks, locked reward pressure, or social comparison.

## Child art direction

### Shape and composition

- Characters use unmistakable silhouettes, large readable eyes/ears/hands, and simple held gestures.
- Scenes use foreground character, one focal action zone, and a quiet background layer. No screen becomes a wall of cards.
- Interactive objects are materially larger and sharper than background objects.
- Corners are friendly but controlled: 16–22 px for major child panels, not fully pill-shaped containers everywhere.
- Use solid-color layering, cut-paper depth, light grain, and crisp key shapes. Avoid gradients, plastic gloss, muddy shadows, and generic blob decoration.

### Proposed palette

| Role | Color | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Deep Harbor Ink | `#123C43` | Primary dark text, outlines, and deep scene anchors |
| Midnight Water | `#0B2F37` | High-contrast controls and dark scene fields |
| Warm Paper | `#FFF8EA` | Light stage fields and support surfaces |
| Shell White | `#FFFDF7` | Small high-clarity interface surfaces |
| Sandstone | `#E8D9BD` | Paths, dock, and neutral props |
| Sky Wash | `#CBE9E6` | Day sky as a solid field |
| Sea Glass | `#84CEC8` | Water planes and quiet secondary accents |
| Miko teal | `#22A8A0` | Miko identity, paired with listening pose or sound mark |
| Pippa coral | `#FF735E` | Pippa identity, paired with gesture silhouette or motion path |
| Moss green | `#759E59` | Moss identity and object/category framing, never generic correctness |
| Nia gold | `#F2C64E` | Nia identity and conversation/story turns, never reward currency |
| Quiet Lavender | `#B7B0E8` | Optional story atmosphere, never instructional state alone |

Color never carries meaning alone. Correctness, availability, support, and selection use shape, icon, motion/pose, and audio in addition to color.

### Line, texture, and light

- Dark colored outlines vary subtly in width and remain crisp at mobile size.
- Texture is low contrast and excluded behind small controls or captions.
- Shadows are short, neutral, and used only to separate interaction layers.
- Scenes use solid atmospheric bands rather than gradients.

### Expression and pose system

Each character needs neutral, speaking, listening, demonstrating, waiting, encouraging, surprised, thinking, pause, and quiet-celebration expressions. “Retry” expressions remain warm and neutral; no disappointment, tears, alarm, or shame.

### Motion and reduced motion

- Functional motions last 180–450 ms; character demonstrations may last up to two seconds and are replayable.
- Idle motion is subtle and stops while audio directions play.
- No confetti storms, camera shake, looping bounce, flashing, or forced parallax.
- Reduced motion replaces travel and gesture animation with two or three held key poses plus a soft focus ring.
- Mouth movement supports attention but never claims phoneme-level scoring accuracy.

### Accessibility

- Child primary touch targets: at least 56 × 56 CSS px with generous separation.
- Large child captions meet at least 3:1 contrast; adult text and smaller labels meet 4.5:1.
- Focus indicators remain visible for keyboard/switch access.
- Every object/scene option has meaningful adult-authored alt text and a non-color semantic ID.
- Recording states use icon, pose, label/audio, and motion—not red/green alone.
- Background detail never obscures the focal gesture or mouth cue.

## Adult-interface direction

This is a separate direction, not a child theme desaturated for adults and not a production rebuild.

### Principles

- Dense, calm, operational, and explicit.
- Labeled navigation always visible at desktop sizes; no icon-only rail as the primary adult navigation.
- Compact tables/timelines for repeated state, with cards reserved for meaningful grouped decisions.
- Neutral warm-gray surfaces, deep ink text, one restrained teal accent, amber for attention, and coral/red only for destructive consequences.
- No gradients, oversized marketing hero panels, decorative arcs, giant empty cards, bubbly pills, or broad soft shadows.
- Border radius normally 6–10 px. Use 2–4 px dividers and status markers where grouping does not require a container.
- Typography prioritizes scanability: 14–16 px operational body, clear tabular numerals, compact labels, and headings sized for hierarchy rather than spectacle.

### Representative adult concept

A parent Week view uses:

- a 216–232 px labeled sidebar with active route, identity, sign out, and provider blockers;
- a compact top bar identifying child, curriculum version, and authoritative week state;
- five horizontal day rows showing objective, targets, lesson format, validation, private approval, assignment/publication, and one state-valid action;
- a right decision inspector showing complete consequences, audit note, preview entry, and recovery action;
- no claim of readiness unless five unique playable assignments and required assets/audio later pass their authoritative checks.

The adult direction must preserve Recovery 1’s separation of private approval, assignment, schedule, publication, completion, replay, replacement, withdrawal, revocation, and archival.

### Registry invariants for Recovery 3

- No generated content reference resolves by arbitrary URL or filename.
- Every child-visible asset version is approved, accessible, and linked to its semantic role.

- Missing, stale, unapproved, or inaccessible assets fail validation and publication.
- An approved asset never silently changes underneath an approved lesson.
- Parent preview renders the exact asset/audio versions Alonso would receive.
